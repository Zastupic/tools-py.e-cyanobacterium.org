Membrane Inlet Mass Spectrometry (MIMS) data analysis

At a glance: Upload MIMS data files to plot dissolved gas signals (O₂, CO₂, and others) over time, normalize them to a chemically inert reference gas (e.g., Ar), and calculate linear regression slopes — giving you net gas exchange rates for photosynthesis, respiration, or other biological processes.

Membrane Inlet Mass Spectrometry (MIMS) is a highly effective analytical technique for the direct, real-time measurement of dissolved gases and volatile organic compounds in a wide variety of samples, including water, biological matrices, and atmospheric gases. A semi-permeable membrane enables selective passage of analytes from the sample into the mass spectrometer while excluding most of the sample matrix — allowing continuous, online monitoring with minimal sample preparation.

Key MIMS advantages ▼
  • Direct, Real-Time Monitoring: ideal for fast-changing biological processes such as photosynthesis and respiration in algal and cyanobacterial cultures without delays from sample preparation.
  • High Sensitivity for Trace Gases: detects gases at sub-parts-per-billion levels — O₂, CO₂, CH₄, and other metabolic gases critical for understanding physiology and productivity.
  • Broad Applicability: supports aqueous cultures, biofilms, sediments — nutrient cycling, gas exchange, environmental stress responses.
  • Continuous & Automated Data Acquisition: perfect for long-term monitoring of dynamic processes like diel cycles and metabolic shifts in cyanobacterial and algal populations.
  • Minimal Sample Preparation: membrane inlet design reduces matrix effects and excludes interfering substances from complex biological media.

Calibration of MIMS signals

Calibration is done by linear regression between measured signal and known concentration: y = a · x + b, where y is the concentration (e.g. µmol L−1), x is the MIMS signal, a is the calibration slope, and b is the intercept.

To convert signal derivatives (regression slopes) to real rates — only the slope a is needed: rate = a · s (the intercept cancels because db/dt = 0). Use the Unit Conversion & Calibration section that appears after fitting regressions.

Equilibrium concentrations of dissolved O₂ and CO₂ in water can be estimated using the dO₂ calculator and dCO₂ calculator.

How to interpret your results ▼
  • A rising O₂ signal (m/z 32) after light onset = net photosynthetic oxygen evolution; the regression slope gives the gross photosynthetic rate. A falling O₂ signal in darkness = respiratory oxygen consumption.
  • A falling CO₂ signal (m/z 44) during illumination indicates net carbon fixation by photosynthesis. A rising CO₂ in the dark = respiratory CO₂ release.
  • Normalization to Ar (m/z 40) is essential — argon is chemically inert, so any change in the Ar signal reflects instrumental drift or temperature fluctuations. Normalizing to Ar removes these artifacts.
  • The linear regression slope over a selected time window gives the gas exchange rate in normalized signal units per minute. Compare slopes between conditions — do not compare raw absolute values between separate runs.
  • To select a regression window: zoom in on the normalized plot to the desired time range, then click Find linear regressions for current zoom.
