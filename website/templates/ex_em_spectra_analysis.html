Fluorescence Spectra & EEM Analyzer

At a glance: Upload 3D excitation-emission fluorescence maps measured at 77 K or room temperature to visualize pigment-protein complex composition and calculate PSII/PSI ratios and phycobilisome coupling states. Supports batch processing of up to 100 files with interactive charts, replicate grouping, and export to .xlsx.

Excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy enables highly sensitive assessment of content, ratios, and interactions of pigment-protein complexes — photosystems (PSI, PSII), phycobilisomes (PBS), and their accessory pigments. At 77 K, biochemical modulation of fluorescence is suppressed, allowing clear discrimination of spectral signatures from individual complexes — essential for assessing stress responses, physiological adaptation, or genetic diversity in cyanobacteria, algae, and higher plants. Room-temperature (RT) EEM measurements provide complementary information on PBS coupling states and PSII:PSI ratios under physiologically relevant conditions.

Gaussian deconvolution

The emission spectrum is fitted as a sum of bell-shaped (Gaussian) curves, one per pigment–protein complex. Each curve is described by three numbers: its height (amplitude), position (emission maximum, in nm), and width (standard deviation, in nm). The area under each curve — height × width × √(2π) — represents the total fluorescence contribution of that complex and is used for all ratio calculations. Using areas rather than peak heights accounts for any differences in peak width between samples or conditions.

Each Gaussian component is assigned to a pigment–protein complex based on its fitted emission maximum (see table below).

PARAFAC (Parallel Factor Analysis)

PARAFAC decomposes the full three-dimensional excitation–emission fluorescence map (EEM) into a set of independent spectral components. Each component has a characteristic excitation spectrum, emission spectrum, and a score (relative abundance) per sample. Components are assigned to pigment–protein complexes in the PARAFAC tab based on their excitation and emission peak positions. Ratios are then computed from the scores of the assigned components.

PARAFAC uses the full EEM and is therefore less sensitive to spectral overlap than single-excitation methods.

Gaussian deconvolution fits a sum of Gaussian-shaped peaks to the emission spectrum recorded at a single fixed excitation wavelength. Each Gaussian represents one fluorescent complex — for example CP43 (~685 nm), CP47 (~695 nm) and PSI (~724 nm) when excited at 440 nm. Because the peaks overlap significantly, fitting the full lineshape separates their contributions more accurately than reading intensity at a single wavelength.

Peak areas (amplitude × σ × √2π), not heights, are used to derive biological parameters, because the area is proportional to the number of emitting molecules regardless of peak width.

When to use it: Gaussian deconvolution is preferred over direct wavelength readings whenever two peaks are separated by less than ~15 nm (e.g. CP43/CP47 at 685/695 nm, or PBS-free/PBS-PSII). It is also the most reliable method for the CP43/CP47 ratio, which PARAFAC cannot resolve due to the small spectral distance between these two complexes.

Limitation: Gaussian lineshapes are approximately valid at 77 K. At room temperature, peaks are broader and less symmetric; R² values are expected to be lower, and results should be interpreted with more caution. Always inspect the R² badge — fits below 0.90 are flagged and should be reviewed manually using the Adjust button.

PARAFAC (Parallel Factor Analysis) decomposes the full excitation-emission matrix (EEM) simultaneously across all uploaded samples. It finds trilinear components — each described by an excitation loading profile, an emission loading profile, and a score (relative abundance) per sample. Each component ideally corresponds to one fluorescent species or energy-transfer pathway (e.g. PBS→PSII, PBS→PSI, uncoupled PBS).

Advantage over single-excitation methods: by using the full EEM, PARAFAC exploits both excitation and emission variation across samples. This makes it particularly powerful for separating PBS coupling states (PBS-free vs PBS→PSII vs PBS→PSI) that appear at the same emission wavelengths but differ in excitation profile. Gaussian deconvolution at a single excitation cannot fully separate these.

Limitation: PARAFAC cannot resolve CP43 and CP47 as separate components — their excitation and emission profiles are too similar (~10 nm apart). For the CP43/CP47 ratio, use the Gaussian deconvolution or direct wavelength method. PARAFAC also requires ≥3 samples with a common EEM grid and assumes all variation across samples is due to changes in component abundance (not peak position).

Recommended workflow:

  1. Run Step 1: Diagnostic (CORCONDIA) to determine the optimal number of components.
  2. Click the optimal F on the CORCONDIA plot to pre-fill Components to fit.
  3. Run Step 2: Run PARAFAC to extract components and scores.
  4. Verify component annotations match expected fluorophores; adjust manually if needed.
  5. Use Copy scores (for method comparison) to compare PARAFAC results with Gaussian and fixed-wavelength parameters.

CORCONDIA (Core Consistency Diagnostic): tests whether the chosen F fits the data in a truly trilinear way. Values near 100% indicate a valid, interpretable model. Values below 80–90% indicate over-factoring — too many components are being extracted and some are noise artefacts. Choose the highest F where CORCONDIA remains ≥ 80–90%.
