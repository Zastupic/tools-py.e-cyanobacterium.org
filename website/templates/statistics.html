P-values and significance
  • p < 0.05 — statistically significant at the conventional α = 0.05 threshold.
  • p < 0.01 — highly significant;  p < 0.001 — very highly significant.
  • A significant p-value does not tell you the size or biological importance of the effect — always inspect the actual data and effect size.
Box plots
  • Box = interquartile range (IQR; 25th–75th percentile). Line inside box = median.
  • Whiskers extend to 1.5 × IQR beyond the box edges. Dots outside whiskers = potential outliers.
  • Individual data points (swarm) show the actual measurements; if N is small, inspect them directly rather than relying only on the box.
Choosing a significance test
  • ANOVA — compares means of 3+ groups; assumes normality (Shapiro–Wilk p > 0.05) and equal variances (Levene p > 0.05).
  • Kruskal–Wallis — non-parametric alternative to ANOVA; use when normality or variance assumptions are violated.
  • Tukey HSD — post-hoc test after ANOVA; shows which specific group pairs differ. Significance letters group means that are not significantly different from each other (same letter = no significant difference).
Assumptions tests
  • Shapiro–Wilk: p > 0.05 = no evidence against normality — the parametric ANOVA is appropriate.
  • Levene: p > 0.05 = variances are homogeneous across groups — the equal-variance ANOVA assumption is met.
  • With small samples (N < 5 per group), these tests have low power — consider using non-parametric tests regardless.

Tests Performed

Before any significance test can be run, the data must satisfy two statistical assumptions required by ANOVA. This section runs those two tests automatically for each selected variable:

1. Shapiro-Wilk (Normality)

Checks if each group follows a Normal Distribution. If p > 0.05, the data is likely normal (Assumption Met).

2. Levene's Test (Homogeneity)

Checks if Variances are equal across groups. If p > 0.05, variances are consistent (Assumption Met).

Next step — ANOVA Tests tab: Results here determine which significance test is most appropriate. In the ANOVA Tests tab you can either let the tool select automatically based on these results, or choose a parametric or non-parametric test manually. The automated logic is: Both passed → One-way ANOVA + Tukey HSD; Only normality → Welch's ANOVA + Games-Howell (BH); Only homogeneity → Kruskal–Wallis + Dunn's (BH); Neither → Kruskal–Wallis + Dunn's (BH).

About this table: The summary below shows the combined results of two statistical assumption tests run for each variable:

  • Shapiro-Wilk (Normality): tests whether the data within each group follows a normal distribution. A p > 0.05 indicates normality is met.

Residuals vs Fitted — how to interpret & outlier removal

Residuals vs Fitted — Primary purpose: Homogeneity of Variances

Displays residuals (observed − group mean) on the Y-axis against fitted values (group means) on the X-axis. Because each group occupies one X position, the vertical spread at each position directly shows within-group variance — making it easy to spot whether spread is consistent across groups (Levene's assumption).

Well-behaved data: Points scattered evenly around zero with similar vertical spread across all groups.
Warning signs: Funnel/fan shape → unequal variances (heteroscedasticity); Curved trend → non-linearity or non-normality; Isolated points far from zero → potential outliers.

Outlier identification (|z| > 2): Raw residuals are standardized (÷ SD) to a dimensionless z-score. Points with |z| > 2 — beyond the ~95% range of a normal distribution — are flagged in red. Before removing, ask: measurement error? → remove; real biological extreme? → keep; many points flagged on one side? → systematic variance issue, removal won't help; does Levene's p change after removal? → re-run to verify.

Click any red point to exclude it permanently and recalculate. Blue points are not clickable.

Normal Q-Q — how to interpret & outlier removal

Normal Q-Q — Primary purpose: Normality of Residuals

Compares the observed quantiles of the residuals (Y-axis) against the theoretical quantiles of a perfect normal distribution (X-axis). If the data is truly normal, both sets of quantiles match and points lie along the diagonal reference line. Deviations reveal exactly where and how the distribution differs from normality.

Well-behaved data: Points lie closely along the diagonal; small deviations at the ends are acceptable.
Warning signs: S-shaped curve → heavy/light tails; one-end bend → skewness; gaps or jumps → bimodality; extreme off-line points → outliers.

Outlier identification (|std residual| > 2): The Y-axis already shows standardized residuals, so the threshold is readable directly from the plot. Points with |std residual| > 2 — beyond the ~95% range expected for normal data — are flagged in red; they also tend to deviate most from the diagonal line. Before removing, ask: measurement error? → remove; real biological extreme? → keep; consistent pattern across many points (S-curve, bend)? → distribution is globally non-normal, removal won't help — consider data transformation; does Shapiro-Wilk p pass after removal? → re-run to verify.

Click any red point to exclude it permanently and recalculate. Blue points are not clickable.

Principal Component Analysis

PCA reduces the dimensionality of your data to show relationships between samples and variables.

What PCA does: PCA finds new axes (principal components, PC1 and PC2) that capture the largest sources of variation in your data. Each sample gets a score on these axes, and the resulting biplot shows both how samples cluster and which original variables drive the separation.

Reading the biplot:

  • Points represent individual samples (rows). Samples that are close together are more similar across all selected variables. Each point's horizontal position is its PC1 score, vertical is PC2.
  • Ellipses show the 95% confidence region for each group (defined by your factor). Drawn at 2 standard deviations of that group's bivariate PC score distribution. Groups whose ellipses overlap are not clearly separated by the selected variables.
  • Arrows (loadings) show how each original variable drives the components. The direction indicates which samples tend to have high values for that variable; the length reflects how strongly that variable contributes. Arrows pointing in similar directions indicate positively correlated variables; opposite directions indicate negative correlation.

All variables are standardised before analysis (mean = 0, unit variance), so variables measured on very different scales contribute equally.

Wide datasets (more variables than samples): Classical PCA requires more samples than variables. This tool uses an SVD-based implementation (scikit-learn) that works correctly even when you have fewer samples than measured variables — the algorithm decomposes the data matrix directly rather than inverting the covariance matrix, so there is no numerical instability. The only hard requirement is at least 2 samples after any filtering.

Missing data strategies — PCA cannot run with any missing values. Four strategies are available:

  • Hybrid (recommended): Variables with more than the threshold % of missing values are excluded from the analysis. The few remaining missing cells are filled with the mean of the same group (or the overall column mean if no factor is defined). No samples are lost.
  • Drop rows: Any sample (row) that has at least one missing value among the selected variables is removed. Simple and conservative, but can lose many samples if missing values are spread across rows.
  • Impute with group / column mean: Every missing value is replaced by the average of that variable within the same factor group (or the overall column mean if no factor is defined). All samples and variables are kept, but imputed values add no real information and slightly compress within-group variation.
  • Exclude variables: Any variable that exceeds the threshold % of missing values is dropped. The remaining variables are used as-is; any rows still missing a value after exclusion are dropped. Keeps all samples but may remove important variables.
  • KNN imputation: Missing values are estimated from the k most similar rows in the dataset. More statistically sophisticated than simple mean imputation, but harder to interpret with very small datasets.

Loading arrows show how strongly each original variable contributes to PC1 and PC2. Arrow length encodes the Euclidean magnitude of the loading in the displayed 2D plane — a long arrow means that variable is well-represented by the two visible components; a short arrow means most of its variance is captured by other PCs not currently shown. Arrows pointing in similar directions indicate correlated variables; opposite directions indicate negative correlation.

Loading arrow controls (available when "Show variable loadings" is checked): Arrow scale stretches or shrinks all arrows visually without changing the underlying values — useful when arrows cluster near the origin or extend beyond the plot. Min. magnitude hides arrows below a threshold (based on 2D Euclidean magnitude, so sign cancellation cannot occur); Top N keeps only the strongest N arrows. Both filters also highlight the corresponding rows in the loadings table shown below the plot.

Explained variance (shown as % on each axis label) tells you how much of the total variability in your dataset is captured by that component. PC1 always captures the most, PC2 the second most.

Orthogonal Projections to Latent Structures (OPLS)

Orthogonal Projections to Latent Structures separates predictive variation (related to Y) from orthogonal variation (unrelated to Y), giving cleaner group discrimination and interpretable S-plots and VIP scores.

OPLS (Orthogonal PLS): a supervised method that decomposes X into two parts — predictive variation (correlated with Y) and orthogonal variation (unrelated to Y, i.e., structured noise). By removing the orthogonal part, the predictive component becomes much cleaner and easier to interpret than in standard PLS.

OPLS-DA (Discriminant Analysis): uses a binary or multi-class group membership vector as Y. It is the most common use case — finding the directions in X that best discriminate between defined groups. Results are displayed as three complementary plots:

  • Score plot (tp vs to): samples plotted on the predictive component (horizontal axis) and the first orthogonal component (vertical axis). Group separation is visible along the horizontal axis; vertical spread represents within-group structured variation that is unrelated to group membership.
  • S-plot: each variable is plotted as its covariance with tp (x-axis) against its correlation with tp (y-axis). Variables in the top-right corner (high covariance and high correlation) are both strongly contributing to group separation and doing so reliably. Variables near the origin contribute little.
  • VIP plot (Variable Importance in Projection): summarises each variable's contribution across all model components. VIP > 1 is the conventional threshold for a variable being important for discrimination. VIP combines information from all predictive and orthogonal components.

Parameters:

  • Orthogonal components: the number of orthogonal (noise) components to remove. Start with 1; increase only if R²X increases substantially without improving Q².
  • Response variable (Y): for OPLS-DA, select a categorical column (group labels); for OPLS, select a continuous outcome variable.
  • Q² (cross-validation): computed by leave-one-out cross-validation. Q² > 0.5 indicates a useful predictive model. A large gap between R²Y and Q² suggests overfitting. Q² close to R²Y is the ideal.

Model metrics — R²X: fraction of total X variance explained by the model. R²Y: fraction of Y variance explained (goodness of fit). Q²: cross-validated predictive ability — the most important metric for assessing whether the model is genuinely predictive rather than just fitting noise.

Partial Least Squares (PLS)

Supervised dimensionality reduction that finds latent variables (LVs) maximising covariance between predictors (X) and a response (Y). PLS-DA uses a categorical Y for group discrimination; PLS uses a continuous Y for regression.

PLS (Partial Least Squares): finds a sequence of latent variables (LVs) that simultaneously decompose X and Y while maximising the covariance between the two. Unlike OPLS, PLS does not separate orthogonal variation — all components contain a mixture of predictive and non-predictive variation.

PLS-DA (Discriminant Analysis): uses binary-encoded class membership as Y. It is directly comparable to OPLS-DA and often gives similar results. PLS-DA can be used with any categorical column.

  • Score plot (T1 vs T2): samples projected onto the first two LV scores. Group separation along T1 indicates LV1 captures between-group variation. Axis labels show per-component R²X contributions.
  • Weights plot (W1 vs W2): each variable shown as an arrow from the origin to its weight coordinates. Variables pointing in similar directions are correlated; large distances from the origin indicate high importance. Arrow colour indicates VIP level (red ≥ 1.5, orange ≥ 1.0, grey < 1.0).
  • VIP plot: Variable Importance in Projection, combining information from all LV components. VIP > 1 is the conventional threshold for importance.

R²X: cumulative X variance explained. R²Y: Y variance explained (goodness of fit). Q²: cross-validated predictive ability — values > 0.5 indicate a useful predictive model. A large R²Y–Q² gap suggests overfitting.

Correlation Analysis

Quantify pairwise linear (or rank) relationships between selected variables. Explore structure with a correlation heatmap and a scatter matrix.

Correlation coefficients quantify the strength and direction of the relationship between two variables. All coefficients range from −1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive), with 0 indicating no relationship.

  • Pearson (r): measures the linear relationship between two continuous variables. Assumes both variables are approximately normally distributed, the relationship is linear, and there are no major outliers. The most statistically powerful choice when its assumptions are met.
  • Spearman (ρ): a rank-based coefficient that measures any monotonic relationship (consistently increasing or decreasing, not necessarily linear). Robust to outliers and non-normal distributions. Use it when data are ordinal or clearly non-normal.
  • Kendall (τ): also rank-based, but counts concordant vs discordant pairs of observations. Slightly less powerful than Spearman in large samples, but more robust for small samples or data with many tied values. τ tends to be numerically smaller than ρ for the same data.

Heatmap: a colour-coded matrix of all pairwise correlation coefficients. Cells are annotated with significance stars: * p<0.05, ** p<0.01, *** p<0.001. Strong positive correlations appear in one colour extreme, strong negative in the other, making clusters of related variables immediately visible.

Scatter matrix: a grid of all pairwise scatter plots. Each cell shows the raw data points for one pair of variables, making it easy to visually assess linearity, outliers, and whether Pearson's assumption of a linear relationship is appropriate. The correlation coefficient r is printed in each cell.

Clustering Analysis

Discover sample groupings using hierarchical clustering, K-Means, or DBSCAN.

Clustering is an unsupervised technique — it finds natural groupings in your data without using any predefined labels. Samples that are similar across the selected variables end up in the same cluster.

Standardising variables (Z-score) is strongly recommended when variables are measured on different scales (e.g., µmol and OD). Without standardisation, variables with large numerical ranges dominate the distance calculation regardless of their biological relevance. Standardisation gives each variable equal weight.

Three methods are available, each with different assumptions: Hierarchical clustering builds a tree of merges and does not require a pre-specified number of clusters; K-Means partitions samples into exactly k groups by minimising within-group variance; DBSCAN finds dense regions and explicitly labels low-density points as noise, making it the only method here that can detect outliers.

Hierarchical clustering builds a dendrogram by successively merging the two closest clusters. Linkage method defines how the distance between two clusters is computed; distance metric defines the distance between individual samples.

Linkage methods:

  • Ward: at each step merges the pair of clusters that produces the smallest increase in total within-cluster variance. Tends to create compact, roughly equal-sized clusters. The most common choice for biological data. Requires Euclidean distance.
  • Complete (maximum): distance between two clusters = distance between their two most dissimilar members. Produces tight, compact clusters and is resistant to outliers within a cluster, but can be sensitive to global outliers.
  • Average (UPGMA): distance = mean of all pairwise distances between members of the two clusters. A balanced compromise — less sensitive to outliers than Complete, less prone to chaining than Single.
  • Single (minimum): distance = distance between the two nearest members across clusters. Can form elongated "chain" clusters where distant samples are linked through a series of close intermediate points. Very sensitive to outliers.

Distance metrics:

  • Euclidean: straight-line distance in multidimensional space (√Σ(xᵢ−yᵢ)²). The standard choice; sensitive to differences in absolute values. Required for Ward linkage.
  • Manhattan (cityblock): sum of absolute differences along each dimension (Σ|xᵢ−yᵢ|). Less sensitive than Euclidean to large differences in individual variables; more robust to outliers.
  • Cosine: measures the angle between two sample vectors, ignoring their magnitude. Two samples are "close" if they have the same pattern of high/low values regardless of overall scale. Useful for compositional or spectral data.
  • Correlation: based on Pearson correlation between two sample vectors (distance = 1 − r). Two samples are close if they have a similar relative profile across all variables, irrespective of mean or scale. Useful for expression or physiological time-course data.

K-Means partitions all samples into exactly k non-overlapping groups by minimising the total within-cluster sum of squares (inertia). The algorithm: (1) randomly initialise k centroids, (2) assign each sample to its nearest centroid, (3) recompute each centroid as the mean of its assigned samples, (4) repeat steps 2–3 until assignments no longer change. The tool runs multiple random initialisations (k-means++) and keeps the best result.

Parameters:

  • Number of clusters (k): the number of groups to find. K-Means always produces exactly k clusters — it cannot detect noise or leave samples unclustered. Must be chosen in advance; use the elbow plot as a guide.
  • Max k for elbow: the maximum k evaluated in the elbow plot. The plot shows within-cluster inertia against k. The optimal k is at the "elbow" — the point where adding an extra cluster gives a much smaller reduction in inertia. Beyond that point, additional clusters capture noise rather than real structure.

Tip: K-Means assumes clusters are roughly spherical and of similar size. If your data has elongated or irregular clusters, hierarchical clustering or DBSCAN may be more appropriate.

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) defines clusters as dense regions of samples separated by low-density areas. Unlike K-Means, it does not require specifying the number of clusters in advance, and it explicitly labels low-density samples as noise (cluster label −1) — making it the only method here capable of detecting outliers.

A sample is a core point if at least min_samples other samples lie within distance ε. Core points form the dense interiors of clusters. Samples reachable from a core point (but not themselves core points) are border points. All remaining samples are noise.

Parameters:

  • ε (epsilon): the neighbourhood radius. Two samples are considered neighbours if their (Euclidean) distance is ≤ ε. Smaller ε creates more, finer clusters and more noise; larger ε merges clusters together. This is the most sensitive parameter. Use the k-distance plot to choose it: set ε at the "knee" of the sorted k-distance curve.
  • Min samples: the minimum number of neighbours within ε for a sample to qualify as a core point. Higher values require denser cores and generally produce fewer but more robust clusters (with more noise points). A common rule of thumb is 2 × (number of variables); 5 is a safe default for general use.
  • k for k-distance: the neighbourhood size used to generate the k-distance plot. Each sample is plotted at its distance to its k-th nearest neighbour, sorted in descending order. The "knee" in this curve is a good starting value for ε. Typically set equal to min_samples − 1, or the same as min_samples.

Tip: start by generating the k-distance plot, identify the knee, and set ε accordingly. Then adjust min_samples to control how strictly cluster membership is defined.

Regression Analysis

Fit and diagnose linear regression models. Predictor variables (X) are taken from the checked variables in the Data Input panel.

Regression models the relationship between one or more predictor variables (X) and a response variable (Y), allowing you to quantify effect sizes, test significance, and make predictions. All linear regression methods here use ordinary least squares (OLS), minimising the sum of squared residuals.

Simple linear regression: models Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε using a single predictor. Outputs a scatter plot with the fitted line and 95% confidence band. Reports R² (fraction of Y variance explained), the slope β₁ with its standard error and p-value, and four diagnostic plots.

Multiple linear regression: extends to several predictors: Y = β₀ + β₁X₁ + … + βₚXₚ + ε. All checked variables are used. Reports Adjusted R² (penalised for adding extra predictors), the overall F-test p-value, individual coefficient estimates and their p-values, and VIF (Variance Inflation Factor) to flag multicollinearity. VIF > 5–10 for a predictor indicates it is highly correlated with other predictors, making individual coefficients unreliable.

Logistic regression: models a binary outcome (0/1) as a probability via the logit link: log(p/(1−p)) = β₀ + Σ βᵢXᵢ. Reports McFadden pseudo-R² (a goodness-of-fit analogue), AUC (area under the ROC curve — 0.5 = no better than chance, 1.0 = perfect discrimination), and a confusion matrix at the default 0.5 probability threshold. Coefficients are log-odds ratios; exponentiating a coefficient gives the odds ratio for a one-unit increase in that predictor.

Diagnostic plots (linear regression):

  • Residuals vs Fitted: checks for non-linearity and heteroscedasticity. Residuals should be randomly scattered around zero with no obvious pattern. A curved pattern suggests a non-linear relationship; a funnel shape suggests variance increases with fitted values.
  • Q-Q plot: checks whether residuals follow a normal distribution. Points should fall close to the diagonal reference line. Systematic deviations indicate non-normality.
  • Scale-Location (√|residuals| vs Fitted): another check for homoscedasticity. A roughly horizontal smoothing line indicates constant variance across the fitted range.
  • Residuals vs Leverage: identifies influential observations. Points outside the dashed Cook's distance lines have a disproportionate influence on the fitted model and are worth investigating individually.

Coefficient plot (forest plot): each predictor is shown as a point estimate (β coefficient) with a horizontal 95% confidence interval. Points to the right of the dashed zero line indicate a positive effect on Y; points to the left indicate a negative effect. If the CI does not cross zero, the predictor is statistically significant at α = 0.05. Coefficients are on the original (unstandardised) scale — their magnitude is only comparable if variables were measured on the same scale.

Residuals vs Fitted: plots raw residuals (observed − predicted) against the fitted values. Ideally, points are scattered randomly around the horizontal zero line with no pattern. A curved trend suggests a non-linear relationship that the model misses; a funnel shape (variance increasing with fitted values) indicates heteroscedasticity, which violates OLS assumptions.

Normal Q-Q: ranks the standardised residuals and plots them against the theoretical quantiles of a normal distribution. Points should fall close to the diagonal reference line. Systematic S-curves or heavy tails indicate non-normality of residuals — a potential OLS assumption violation, though the model is quite robust to mild departures with n > 30.

Scale-Location (√|residuals| vs Fitted): the square root of the absolute standardised residuals against fitted values. A roughly horizontal smoothing line indicates homoscedasticity (constant error variance). An upward slope means variance grows with fitted values; a downward slope means the opposite.

Residuals vs Leverage: identifies influential observations — those that have a disproportionate effect on the fitted model. Leverage measures how far a sample's X values are from the mean of X. Cook's distance contours (dashed lines) mark thresholds; points outside these lines (typically Cook's D > 0.5 or 1) are worth investigating individually. High leverage alone is not a problem — it becomes one only when combined with a large residual.
