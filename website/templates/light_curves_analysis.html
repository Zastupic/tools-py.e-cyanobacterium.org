Rapid Light Curve Analyzer (Photosynthesis-Irradiance curves)

At a glance: Upload rapid light curve data from your PAM fluorometer to plot electron transport rate (rETR) vs. irradiance and extract key photosynthetic parameters — including ETRmax, photosynthetic efficiency (α), saturation irradiance (Ik), and the photoinhibition coefficient (β). Welcome to the rapid light curves analyzing tool! Rapid light curves (also called photosynthesis-irradiance curves) are a widely used method for assessing photosynthetic performance under varying light intensities. By analyzing how chlorophyll fluorescence changes in response to stepwise increases in actinic light, this method provides insights into photosynthetic efficiency, photoprotection mechanisms, and the functional state of photosystem II (PSII). The rapid light curves can be measured in plants, algae, cyanobacteria and in some photosynthetic bacteria.

The curves are fitted by the model described by Platt et al. (1980):

$$\begin{multline}\shoveleft rETR = rETR_{mPot} (1 - e^\frac{\alpha \cdot PAR}{rETR_{mPot}}) e^{-\frac{\beta \cdot PAR}{rETR_{mPot}}}\end{multline}$$

  • ETRmax — maximum relative electron transport rate. Typical unstressed cyanobacteria/algae: 50–200 µmol e⁻ m⁻² s⁻¹; higher values indicate greater photosynthetic capacity.
  • α — initial slope of the light curve; photosynthetic efficiency under weak light. Higher α = better use of dim light. Typical range: 0.2–0.8.
  • Ik — saturation irradiance (light level where ETR saturates). Low-light adapted: 50–200 µmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹; high-light adapted: 200–600+ µmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹.
  • β — photoinhibition coefficient. β > 0 indicates a measurable decline in ETR at high irradiance (photoinhibition); β ≈ 0 means no significant photoinhibition was detected.
  • NPQ — non-photochemical quenching; a measure of photoprotective thermal dissipation. Values > 1 indicate active photoprotection; > 3 suggests strong dissipation under stress.
