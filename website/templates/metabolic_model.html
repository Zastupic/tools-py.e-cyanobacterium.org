Flux Balance Analysis of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803

The genome-scale metabolic reconstruction of the cyanobacterium Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 (Höper et al., 2024, PLoS Comput. Biol.) encompasses 920 reactions, 809 metabolites, and 783 genes. It builds upon earlier reconstructions by Knoop et al. (2013) and Knoop & Steuer (2015), with revised gene-protein-reaction associations, the addition of the Entner-Doudoroff pathway, and updated coverage of purine, pyrimidine, and biotin metabolism. The model incorporates a novel description of light absorption and utilization within the FBA framework, including a variable quantum yield and photoinhibition. Model parameters were calibrated against quantitative growth data obtained in a light-limited turbidostat (Zavřel et al., 2019, eLife), which provides the primary experimental reference dataset including growth rates, O2 evolution, and biomass composition as a function of light intensity.

Metabolites and reactions are organized into 7 compartments that reflect the cellular ultrastructure of Synechocystis (see figure):

  • e — extracellular space (growth medium with dissolved nutrients)
  • p — periplasm (space between outer and cytoplasmic membranes)
  • cm — cytoplasmic membrane (respiratory complexes, nutrient transporters)
  • c — cytoplasm (central metabolism, anabolic reactions)
  • cx — carboxysome (CO2-concentrating microcompartment with RuBisCO)
  • um — thylakoid membrane (photosynthetic electron transport: PSII, Cyt b6f, PSI, ATPase)
  • u — thylakoid lumen (proton gradient driving ATP synthesis)

How to use this tool

iRH783 is a genome-scale metabolic reconstruction of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803. Flux Balance Analysis (FBA) finds the intracellular flux distribution that maximises biomass synthesis rate, subject to stoichiometric mass balance, reaction directionality, and user-set exchange boundaries (nutrient and photon uptake rates); the result includes a predicted specific growth rate for the chosen conditions (Static FBA tab). Photon uptake is corrected for quantum-yield saturation: J*I = KL·JI / (KL + JI). A biophysical layer (Höper 2024) couples the FBA-derived biomass yield on photons (YBM) with a Lambert-Beer light-attenuation model over areal biomass density (XA), photodamage (kd), and maintenance (NGAM) to predict growth rate across the full range of light intensities (FBA Light Response tab) and simulate time-course culture productivity (Productivity tab).

What does static FBA do?
Static FBA finds the optimal flux distribution for the current medium composition and I₀ — giving the stoichiometric maximum growth rate at that single operating point. The photon uptake bound uses the quantum-yield correction J*I = KL × JI / (KL + JI) (Höper 2024), so the result is already biologically constrained and close to experimental values. Use this tab to inspect individual reaction fluxes, identify active pathways, visualise on KEGG maps, and compare wild-type vs. knockout configurations. For how growth rate scales with light across the full range, proceed to Tab ②.

How to read the chemostat chart
Optimal dilution rate (Dopt)
Dilution rate D (h⁻¹) at which areal biomass productivity PA is maximised (peak of the green curve). D > Dopt: culture washout — cells are removed faster than they grow. D < Dopt: self-shading increases and productivity declines.
Optimal areal biomass density (ρA,opt)
Steady-state areal biomass density (g CDM·m⁻²) at Dopt — shown on the orange curve (right axis). This is the target operating density that balances light penetration and biomass concentration. Running denser increases self-shading; running sparser wastes light.
Maximum areal biomass productivity (PA,max)
Maximum biomass produced per unit panel area per day, achieved at Dopt. Vary the I₀ slider to find the irradiance at which PA,max is highest — it rises with I₀ until photoinhibition (governed by kd) causes it to fall.
Metabolic engineering headroom (YBM → FBA ceiling)
The FBA stoichiometric ceiling (1.84 gCDW·mmol⁻¹) is the theoretical maximum YBM set by the metabolic network. Raising YBM toward this value in the slider shows the predicted PA,max after metabolic engineering.

How to read the batch chart

Peak areal productivity (PA,max)
Maximum instantaneous areal biomass productivity (g CDM·m⁻²·h⁻¹) during the batch — reached at the time when the balance between growth rate and biomass density is optimal, before self-shading begins to dominate.
Back-face irradiance (Ibottom)
Irradiance transmitted through the culture to the back panel face (yellow dashed, right axis of the density chart). When Ibottom → 0, the culture interior is light-limited and growth rate declines steeply. A practical reactor target is Ibottom ≥ 10–20 µmol·m⁻²·s⁻¹.
Growth rate decline (μ)
Specific growth rate (h⁻¹, blue curve, right axis) declines monotonically as biomass accumulates and self-shading increases. The culture does not reach a true steady state — harvest before growth rate approaches zero to maximise volumetric productivity.
Initial biomass density effect
Increase ρA,0 (left sidebar) to simulate a higher inoculum. Higher ρA,0 accelerates entry into the self-shaded phase and shifts PA,max earlier in time, at the cost of a lower peak growth rate.

How is the biosynthetic cost calculated?

The tool uses Flux Balance Analysis (FBA) on the genome-scale metabolic model of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 to estimate the minimum photosynthetic resources (photons, CO₂, ATP, NADPH) required to synthesise one millimole of a chosen product from inorganic precursors.

Step-by-step procedure

  1. Photoautotrophic model. The constrained model is used (no organic carbon, photons + CO₂ + NO₃⁻ as sole inputs), with the forced minimum photon uptake removed so the solver is free to use exactly as many photons as the target synthesis requires.
  2. Export drain added. A "demand" reaction that irreversibly consumes the target metabolite (exporting it from the system) is added or identified. This prevents the cell from incorporating the product into biomass, so the cost reflects pure synthesis, not growth.
  3. Objective: maximise product export. FBA maximises the drain flux — the rate at which the target molecule is produced and exported.
  4. pFBA minimises total flux. Parsimonious FBA (pFBA) then finds the flux distribution with the smallest total absolute flux that still achieves the maximum export rate. This eliminates thermodynamically infeasible futile cycles and gives the most direct biosynthetic route.
  5. Normalisation. Every exchange flux (photons, CO₂, O₂, NO₃⁻) and every ATP/NADPH balance is divided by the resulting product export flux, giving per-molecule costs.

FBA vs pFBA

Both solutions are shown side by side. FBA may include parallel paths with equal efficiency (degenerate solutions). pFBA is parsimonious — it selects the single most direct route. For biosynthetic cost estimation, pFBA values are typically more meaningful because they suppress futile cycles.

Independent vs Based-on-simulation modes

  • Independent: growth is left unconstrained but receives no resources beyond what is strictly required for the target pathway (pFBA minimises growth to near zero). This gives the theoretical minimum cost of synthesising the molecule from CO₂ and light.
  • Based on simulation: the biomass reaction is fixed at the growth rate from the most recent chemostat simulation. The per-molecule cost then reflects synthesis within a growing cell at that specific dilution rate.

Pathway / subsystem breakdown

Reactions are grouped by their annotated pathway (using the model's internal prefix codes: PP = Calvin cycle, GS = glycolysis, AA = aromatic amino acids, etc.). For each group the net ATP and NADPH balance is reported — positive means the pathway produces that currency (e.g. photosynthetic reactions generate ATP), negative means it consumes it. The sum across all pathways is zero by mass balance.

Caveats

  • FBA is a steady-state, constraint-based model. It does not capture kinetics, enzyme capacities, or regulatory effects.
  • The result is a lower bound on the true in-vivo cost; actual cells have thermodynamic inefficiencies and maintenance requirements not fully captured here.
  • Costs depend on which metabolic route the solver selects. If multiple routes are equally efficient, pFBA picks one arbitrarily; the true in-vivo route may differ.
