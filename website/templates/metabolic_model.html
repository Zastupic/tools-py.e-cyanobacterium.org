Metabolic Reconstruction of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803

The genome-scale metabolic reconstruction iRH783 of the cyanobacterium Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 (Höper et al., 2024, PLoS Comput. Biol.) encompasses 920 reactions, 809 metabolites, and 783 genes. It builds upon earlier reconstructions by Knoop et al. (2013) and Knoop & Steuer (2015), with revised gene-protein-reaction associations, the addition of the Entner-Doudoroff pathway, and updated coverage of purine, pyrimidine, and biotin metabolism. The model incorporates a novel description of light absorption and utilization within the FBA framework, including a variable quantum yield and photoinhibition. Model parameters were calibrated against quantitative growth data obtained in a light-limited turbidostat (Zavřel et al., 2019, eLife), which provides the primary experimental reference dataset including growth rates, O2 evolution, and biomass composition as a function of light intensity.

Metabolites and reactions are organized into 7 compartments that reflect the cellular ultrastructure of Synechocystis (see figure):

  • e — extracellular space (growth medium with dissolved nutrients)
  • p — periplasm (space between outer and cytoplasmic membranes)
  • cm — cytoplasmic membrane (respiratory complexes, nutrient transporters)
  • c — cytoplasm (central metabolism, anabolic reactions)
  • cx — carboxysome (CO2-concentrating microcompartment with RuBisCO)
  • um — thylakoid membrane (photosynthetic electron transport: PSII, Cyt b6f, PSI, ATPase)
  • u — thylakoid lumen (proton gradient driving ATP synthesis)

What this tab shows
Static FBA finds the optimal flux distribution for the current medium composition and I₀ — giving the stoichiometric maximum growth rate at that single operating point. The photon uptake bound uses the quantum-yield correction J*I = KL × JI / (KL + JI) (Höper 2024), so the result is already biologically constrained and close to experimental values. Use this tab to inspect individual reaction fluxes, identify active pathways, visualise on KEGG maps, and compare wild-type vs. knockout configurations. For how growth rate scales with light across the full range, proceed to Tab ②.

What this tab shows
FBA light sweep runs FBA across a range of photon fluxes (Imin → Imax) and plots the stoichiometric maximum growth rate, biomass yield per photon, and O₂ evolution. Growth rates here are theoretical ceilings — real cultures fall below these values due to photodamage and maintenance costs, which are quantified in Tab ③. Key use: click FBA sweep → Fit YBM → Update model in Tab ③ to calibrate the kinetic model to the stoichiometric ceiling for your specific metabolic configuration (medium, knockouts, heterologous pathways). The green Höper curve and experimental data are overlaid automatically once a sweep has been run.

What this tab shows
Kinetically realistic growth rate prediction using the Höper 2024 model. Unlike the FBA ceiling (Tab ②), this model includes: quantum yield saturation (KL — photosystems saturate at high light), photodamage (kd — a fraction of absorbed photons damages PSII rather than driving growth), and maintenance costs (NGAMphoton — energy required just to stay alive). How to use: Run the FBA light sweep in Tab ② for your metabolic scenario, then click FBA sweep → Fit YBM → Update model below to calibrate the kinetic model to your specific configuration. The fitted green curve should align closely with the Zavřel 2019 experimental data (blue dots, wild-type Synechocystis PCC 6803). What to expect: Growth rate peaks around 440–660 µmol·m⁻²·s⁻¹ then declines due to photoinhibition. The gap between the FBA ceiling (red dots) and Höper curve (green line) represents the photodamage and maintenance overhead quantified by k_d and NGAM_photon.
What this tab shows
Predicts areal biomass productivity P_A (gCDM·m⁻²·d⁻¹) for a flat-panel photobioreactor using the calibrated Höper 2024 kinetic model. Self-shading (τ = α·X_A) limits light penetration through the culture — productivity peaks at an optimal biomass density, then declines as the culture becomes too dense. Chemostat: At steady state, dilution rate D = growth rate μ. Productivity peaks at D_opt (lower than in heterotrophic cultures due to light limitation). Use this to find the optimal operating point. Batch: Growth rate decelerates as biomass accumulates and self-shading increases. The simulation shows the trade-off between fast initial growth and the eventual light-limited plateau. Parameters are set on the left: I₀ sets incident irradiance, XA sets initial/operating areal biomass density. Biophysical parameters (α, kd etc.) are configured in Tab ③.
No net growth at current parameters — reduce kd or increase I₀.
